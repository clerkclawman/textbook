## 1. Overview

### Curriculum Summary

**‚ö†Ô∏è CRITICAL DESIGN PRINCIPLE: NO PROGRESSION**

**All 48 stories are at the SAME Grade 1 difficulty level.** No progressive building to "mastery."

**Why No Progression:**
- Students can join the level at ANY week (Week 1, Week 25, Week 45, etc.)
- Students read stories in order starting from the week they join
- Week 1 must be the same level as Week 48
- Each story is self-contained

- Fair rotation of ALL hedging types (epistemic, attribution, modal-based, complex)
- Fair rotation of ALL discourse types (addition, contrast, consequence, conclusion)
- Different topics for variety (education, research, AI, climate, economics, etc.)
- NO "integration phase" or "mastery phase"

**Every Single Story (Weeks 1-48) Must Have:**
- Hedging markers: 3-6 instances (flexible range)
- Discourse markers: 4-6 instances (minimum 4)
- Academic passive: 5-8 sentences (MANDATORY)
- Sentence length: 10-15 words (max 20)
- Japanese translations: ALL 20 lines

## 2. Grammar Variety by Month (NO PROGRESSION - FAIR ROTATION)

### Fair Rotation Strategy

**ALL hedging types and discourse markers are available EVERY month.**

No monthly isolation. No "Month X MUST use Type A." Instead:
- Story writers choose from ALL hedging types (epistemic, attribution, modal-based, complex)
- Story writers choose from ALL discourse types (addition, contrast, consequence, conclusion)
- **FAIR ROTATION:** Across 48 stories, ensure ALL types appear regularly and frequently

### Available Hedging Markers (Use Freely)

| Type | Markers | Examples |
|------|---------|----------|
| **Epistemic** | arguably, perhaps, ostensibly, apparently, seemingly, likely, presumably | Arguably, this suggests... |
| **Attribution** | reportedly, purportedly, allegedly, supposedly | Reportedly, the study shows... |
| **Modal-based** | may, might, could, would | This may indicate... |
| **Complex** | arguably + reportedly, apparently + supposedly, may + arguably | Arguably, reportedly the data suggests... |

### Available Discourse Markers (Use Freely)

| Type | Markers |
|------|---------|
| **Addition** | moreover, furthermore, in addition, additionally |
| **Contrast** | however, nevertheless, on the other hand, conversely |
| **Consequence** | therefore, consequently, as a result, thus |

### Fair Rotation Guidance

**For every 4-story batch:**
- Include at least 2-3 different hedging types
- Include at least 2-3 different discourse types
- Avoid repeating the SAME type 4 times in a row

**Example (Weeks 1-4):**
- Story 1: Epistemic hedges + Contrast discourse
- Story 2: Attribution hedges + Consequence discourse
- Story 3: Modal hedges + Addition discourse
- Story 4: Complex hedges + Conclusion discourse

**This ensures variety AND allows writers flexibility.**

## 3. Grammar Requirements per Story (CRITICAL)

### Hedging Markers: 3-6 Instances (Flexible Range)
- **Target:** 3-6 instances per story  
- **Focus:** Quality over strict count ‚Äî use hedges naturally and effectively  
- **Flexible:** 4 or 5 is fine; don't obsess over exact numbers

### Discourse Markers: 4-6 Instances (STRICT MINIMUM = 4)
- **Target:** 4-6 instances per story  
- **Minimum:** 4 discourse markers required (do NOT use fewer than 4)  
- **Maximum:** 6 discourse markers to avoid over-use

### Academic Passive: Lines 1-5 FORCED PASSIVE (MANDATORY)

**CRITICAL REQUIREMENT (FIXED FOR RECURRING FAILURE):**

Natural language training makes models prefer ACTIVE voice. Instructions like "include 5-8 passive" are repeatedly ignored.

**SOLUTION: FORCE PASSIVE IN SPECIFIC LINES**

```
Line 1: MUST BE PASSIVE ("was/were [verb]ed" or "is/are [verb]ed")
Line 2: MUST BE PASSIVE
Line 3: MUST BE PASSIVE
Line 4: MUST BE PASSIVE
Line 5: MUST BE PASSIVE

Lines 6-10: Can be ACTIVE or PASSIVE (no restriction)
Lines 11-20: Questions ‚Äî Can be ACTIVE or PASSIVE (no restriction)
```

**Example (CORRECT):**
- Line 1: The study was conducted at the university. (PASSIVE ‚úÖ MANDATORY)
- Line 2: Data were collected from sources. (PASSIVE ‚úÖ MANDATORY)
- Line 3: Results have been published. (PASSIVE ‚úÖ MANDATORY)
- Line 4: The theory is supported. (PASSIVE ‚úÖ MANDATORY)
- Line 5: Methods are being developed. (PASSIVE ‚úÖ MANDATORY)
- Line 6: Researchers continue work. (ACTIVE ‚úì OK)
- Line 7: Funding has been secured. (PASSIVE ‚úì OK)

**Result:** Lines 1-5 = 5 PASSIVE MINIMUM (FORCED), requirement met ‚úÖ

**Passive Patterns (use these):**
- Past passive: "was/were + [verb]ed"
- Present passive: "is/are + [verb]ed"
- Perfect passive: "has/have been + [verb]ed"

**DO NOT use active in Lines 1-5:**
- ‚ùå "Researchers conducted the study." (active)
- ‚ùå "The journal published the research." (active)
- ‚úÖ "The study was conducted." (passive)
- ‚úÖ "The research was published." (passive)

### All Sentences: 10-15 Words Maximum (TV-Readable Constraint)
- **Average:** 10-15 words per sentence  
- **Maximum:** 20 words per sentence (hard limit)  
- **Apply to:** ALL 20 lines (both story sentences AND discussion questions)

### Grammar Complexity Strategy
Use 2-3 short sentences with discourse markers instead of deep embedding:

**Before (too long):** *"Research suggests that studies have shown that evidence supports the view that climate change is accelerating."* (19 words)

**After (TV-readable):** *"Research suggests climate change is accelerating. This evidence is strong."* (9 + 5 = 14 words)

### Academic Passive Examples by Tense
- **Present passive:** *"It is argued that policy reforms are needed."* (general truths)  
- **Past passive:** *"The study was conducted over three years."* (research completed)  
- **Perfect passive:** *"Data have been analysed thoroughly."* (current relevance)  
- **Future passive:** *"Results will be published next month."* (predictions)

## 5. Content Quality Guidelines

### Primary Priority: Good, Readable Content
- **Above all:** Stories should be engaging and well-written  
- **Readability:** 10-15 word sentences ensure TV readability with translations  
- **Academic tone:** Professional but not dry, formal but accessible  
- **Topic relevance:** Academic/professional themes (education, research, policy, AI, climate, economics, etc.)

### Secondary Priority: Humour and Engagement
**Use humour where appropriate to make content memorable:**

**Appropriate humour themes:**
- Academic bureaucracy delays and absurdities
- Perfect research plans that inevitably change
- Student procrastination and deadline madness
- Group project dynamics (everyone loves group work)
- Conference presentation anxiety
- Reviewer feedback being politely brutal

**Humour principles:**
- Gentle wit that students will recognise
- Irony about real academic situations
- Playful commentary students relate to
- **Avoid:** Sarcasm, cynicism, mocking, or mean-spirited content

**When to use humour:**
- Opening lines can grab attention
- Examples can be playful
- Conclusions can warm the tone  
- **When to avoid:** Serious topics like inequality, discrimination, tragedy

### Tertiary Priority: Seasonal References (OPTIONAL, Not Required)
**Seasonal content is OPTIONAL ‚Äî do not force it**

**If using seasonal references:**
- Mention seasonal events only when they naturally relate to the topic
- Ensure seasonal events appear in their correct month:
  - Cherry blossoms: March-April (Japan)
  - Christmas: December
  - Halloween: October
- Don't force seasonal connections that feel artificial

**Better non-seasonal than weak seasonal:**
- ‚úÖ Excellent non-seasonal content about AI ethics  
- ‚ùå Weak seasonal reference about Christmas in July (forced and confusing)

### Topic Flexibility
**Stories can be about ANY academic/professional topic:**
- Education and research
- Technology and AI
- Climate and environment
- Economics and business
- International relations
- Health and medicine
- Law and policy
- Philosophy and ethics

**Quality principle:** Theme flexibility allows creators to choose topics they can write well about
