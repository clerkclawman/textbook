## 1. Level Overview ‚Äî Grade 4 Summary

### Profile
- **Official Description:** Simple English for basic communication
- **Test Reality:** Past/future tenses with reading passages ‚Äî **basic-intermediate, not "simple"**
- **Vocabulary Scope:** 937 unique words (71% jump from 5Á¥ö's 558)
- **Sentence Length:** 12-14 words average (TV-readable with translations)
- **Proficiency Level:** Lower-intermediate ‚Äî foundational past/future skills
- **Critical Constraint:** Sentences must be TV-readable with Japanese translations displayed simultaneously

### Critical Transition: 5Á¥ö ‚Üí 4Á¥ö

This is the **1st major transition point** in the Eiken progression:

| Metric | 5Á¥ö | 4Á¥ö | Change | Significance |
|--------|-----|-----|--------|--------------|
| Vocabulary | 558 | 937 | +71% | **Largest % jump of any level transition** |
| Sentence length | 10-12 words | 12-14 words | +20% | Reading passages increase complexity |
| **Past simple** | Minimal | **Major feature** (36 occurrences) | NEW | Core skill for Grade 4 |
| **Future with will** | Minimal | **Major feature** (29 occurrences) | NEW | Core skill for Grade 4 |
| **Comparatives** | Minimal | **Major feature** | NEW | Core skill for Grade 4 |
| Reading passages | Single sentences | **Multi-sentence passages** | NEW | Reading comprehension becomes critical |

**Note on Sentence Length:** Whilst actual Grade 4 exams use slightly shorter sentences than this curriculum, we use 12-14 words to ensure TV readability with translations. Grammar complexity is maintained through past/future tenses and comparative structures.

### What Makes Grade 4 Different

**Unlike 5Á¥ö:**
1. **Past tense mastery** ‚Äî Can narrate events that happened
2. **Future planning** ‚Äî Can discuss plans and predictions
3. **Comparison skills** ‚Äî Can compare items, people, places
4. **Reading passages** ‚Äî Multi-sentence texts require comprehension
5. **Vocabulary expansion** ‚Äî 71% increase in required vocabulary

**Continuing from 5Á¥ö:**
- Present continuous remains useful
- Basic sentence structure (SVO)
- Question forms (Wh-questions, yes/no)

## 2. Test Reality Summary ‚Äî What Eiken Analysis Shows

### The Official Description vs. Test Reality Gap

| Official Description | Test Reality | Impact on Teaching |
|---------------------|--------------|-------------------|
| "Simple English" | **Past simple, future with will, and comparatives are the core differentiators** | Teach time concepts, not just "basic communication" |
| "Basic situations" | **Reading passages with multi-sentence texts** | Students need comprehension skills, not just isolated sentences |
| "Elementary vocabulary" | **937 words required (71% jump from 5Á¥ö)** | Vocabulary expansion is critical |

### Four Pillars of Grade 4 Success

#### 1. Past Simple Tense (CRITICAL ‚Äî Core Skill)

**What the data shows:**
- Past simple occurs 36 times in Grade 4 exams
- This is the **first major grammar feature** tested extensively
- Regular and irregular past forms both important
- Time expressions crucial (*yesterday, last week, ago*)

**Test evidence:**
- 5Á¥ö: Past tense minimal (focus on present continuous)
- 4Á¥ö: Past simple dominant (36 occurrences)

#### 2. Future with Will (Core Skill)

**Key uses:**
- Predictions (*It will rain tomorrow*)
- Spontaneous decisions (*I'll help you*)
- Promises (*I'll call you later*)
- Future time expressions (*next week, soon, in the future*)

**Frequency:** 29 occurrences in Grade 4 exams

#### 3. Comparative Structures (Core Skill)

**Key forms:**
- Comparatives with -er/more (*bigger, more expensive*)
- Superlatives with -est/most (*biggest, most expensive*)
- Comparative phrases (*as...as*, *not as...as*)
- Quantifiers for comparison (*more than, less than*)

**Test evidence:**
- Comparatives are introduced at Grade 4
- Essential for comparing people, places, things

#### 4. Reading Comprehension (New Feature)

**What's different from 5Á¥ö:**
- 5Á¥ö: Single-sentence questions
- 4Á¥ö: **Multi-sentence passages** requiring comprehension
- Inference and detail retrieval required
- Questions ask about passage content, not just isolated sentences

### The "Simple English" Misconception

**Current curriculum gap:**
- Many resources treat Grade 4 as "very basic"
- The 71% vocabulary jump and reading passages suggest otherwise
- Grade 4 is **basic-intermediate**, not beginner

**Evidence from analysis:**
- 5Á¥ö ‚Üí 4Á¥ö is the **largest % vocabulary jump** (71%) of ANY transition
- Reading passages introduce significant complexity
- Three major grammar features (past, future, comparatives) all emerge at this level

## 6. Seasonal & Cultural Context

### Overview

This section provides seasonal and cultural context for the Grade 4 curriculum. Seasonal references are **OPTIONAL**, not mandatory.

**Primary Goals:**
1. **Good, readable content** ‚Äî Most important
2. **Engaging, relatable material** ‚Äî Humour is ideal when appropriate
3. **Seasonal alignment** ‚Äî Optional; if included, must align to correct month

**For Grade 4:** Stories can be about ANY age-appropriate topic (school, hobbies, family, travel, sports, etc.). Seasonal events are nice extras when they fit naturally, but should never be forced.

**Content Quality Principles:**
- Better to have excellent non-seasonal content than weak seasonal references
- Humour and engagement are more valuable than seasonal accuracy
- Students learn better when content is interesting and relatable
- Seasonal events create memorable context when used naturally, but not required

### Seasonal Alignment Policy (SIMPLIFIED)

**Seasonal Content: OPTIONAL, Not Required**

Stories may include seasonal references, but this is not mandatory:
- **Primary Priority:** Good, readable, engaging content
- **Secondary Priority:** Humour and wit make content memorable
- **Tertiary Priority:** Seasonal alignment (if relevant to topic)

**When Using Seasonal References:**
- Mention seasonal events only when they naturally relate to the topic
- Ensure seasonal events appear in their correct month:
  - Cherry blossoms: March-April (Japan)
  - Christmas: December
  - Halloween: October
  - Setsubun/Tanabata/Obon: Correct months in Japan
- Don't force seasonal connections that feel artificial

**Examples of Acceptable Seasonal References:**
- December story about holiday plans ‚Üí mentions Christmas or New Year
- April story about school ‚Üí mentions new school year starting
- June story about weather ‚Üí mentions rainy season
- October story about parties ‚Üí mentions Halloween

**Examples of Unforced (Non-Seasonal) Stories:**
- Sports and hobbies (any month)
- Family trips (any month)
- School subjects and clubs (any month)
- Music, movies, and entertainment (any month)

**Humour Integration (IDEAL):**
Where appropriate, include subtle wit or humour:
- Playful situations students recognize (forgot homework, failed tests made funny)
- Relatable family situations
- Funny mishaps during activities
- Avoid sarcasm or cynicism ‚Äî keep it warm and relevant

### Cultural Awareness Guidelines

#### 1. Age-Appropriate Content

**Grade 4 level topics:**
- School life and education
- Hobbies and interests
- Family and friends
- Travel and experiences
- Sports and activities
- Simple observations about daily life

**What to avoid:**
- Complex political topics
- Advanced economic concepts
- Abstract philosophical discussions
- Topics requiring sophisticated vocabulary

#### 2. International References (Simple, Clear)

**Use simple comparisons:**
- "In Japan, we celebrate New Year on January 1st. In America, they celebrate New Year too."
- "Cherry blossoms bloom in Japan during spring. In England, flowers bloom in spring too."

**Keep it simple:**
- 1-2 sentences about other regions maximum
- Focus on similarities, not complex differences
- Use familiar vocabulary (937-word scope)

#### 3. Avoiding Stereotypes

**What to avoid:**
- ‚ùå "Japanese people always eat rice"
- ‚ùå "American children eat hamburgers every day"

**What to use:**
- ‚úÖ "In Japan, many people eat rice with meals."
- ‚úÖ "In America, hamburgers are a popular food."

**Key principle:** Use specific, accurate details rather than broad generalisations.

**Note:** These targets are for individual sentences (lines 1-10), not total story word count. Each story contains exactly 20 lines.

### Sentence Length Strategy

**How to structure Grade 4 sentences:**

1. **Simple sentences with past/future tenses**
   - "I went to the park yesterday." (7 words)
   - "She will study English tomorrow." (6 words)

2. **Compound sentences with basic conjunctions**
   - "I like summer, but I don't like hot days." (11 words)
   - "He studied hard, and he got a good score." (10 words)

3. **Comparative sentences**
   - "This book is more interesting than that one." (10 words)
   - "She is taller than her brother." (7 words)

4. **Use time expressions appropriately**
   - "Last winter, we visited Hokkaido." (6 words)
   - "Next month, I will join the tennis club." (9 words)

### Tone and Engagement (IDEAL Quality Factors)

**Primary Tone:** Friendly, encouraging, relatable ‚Äî simple but not childish

**Humour (Ideal to Include Where Appropriate):**
- Use gentle situations students will recognise
- Playful moments in everyday life
- Relatable school or family situations
- **Avoid:** Sarcasm, cynicism, or unkind humor

**Engagement Characteristics:**
- Relatable topics students experience
- Memorable examples that make grammar clear
- Warm, encouraging tone
- Humour should be light and inclusive

**Example Humour Themes (Grade 4 appropriate):**

**When to Use Humour:**
- Opening lines can grab attention
- Examples can be playful

- **When to avoid:** Serious topics like sadness, disappointment

### Complexity Requirements

#### Clause Structure

| Requirement | Target | Example (TV-readable) |
|-------------|--------|----------------------|
| **Simple sentences** | 40-50% of all sentences | Single independent clause |
| **Compound sentences** | 30-40% of all sentences | Two independent clauses with 'and/but/or' |
| **Complex sentences** | 10-20% of all sentences | One independent + one dependent clause |
| **Subordinate clause types** | 1-2 types per story | Because, when, if clauses |

**Key Strategy:** Use simple and compound sentences primarily. Introduce complex sentences gradually with familiar subordinate conjunctions.

**Example Transformation:**
- *Before (complex):* "When I went to the park yesterday with my friends who I met at school, we played football until it started to rain." (24 words ‚Äî too long)
- *After (TV-readable):* "Yesterday, I went to the park. I met my friends at school. We played football together. It started to rain later." (6 + 7 + 5 + 5 words = 4 sentences)

### Step 2: Generate Content Based on Plan

**After plan is complete:**

1. Worker agent generates stories following the plan
2. Reviewer agent checks stories against plan
3. If approved: Save and continue
4. If rejected: Fix, retry

### Step 3: Quality Review and Approval

**Every story MUST pass:**

- [ ] Plan requirements followed
- [ ] Japanese translations on ALL 20 lines
- [ ] Grammar distribution correct for story type
- [ ] Sentence length: 12-14 words (max 20)
- [ ] Past/Future stories have 3-5 time expressions
- [ ] Comparative stories have required comparative structures

```

2. Plan (TEXTBOOK-PLAN) ‚Üê FIRST STEP (not done yet)
```

**Critical Rule:** NEVER generate content without a plan document first. The plan is the single source of truth for what each story must contain.

### NO PROGRESSION Principle (Critical Design)

**All 48 stories are at the SAME Grade 4 difficulty level:**

- Students can join the level at ANY week (Week 1, Week 25, Week 45, etc.)
- Students read stories in order starting from the week they join
- Week 1 must be the same level as Week 48
- Each story is self-contained

**Weekly Changes = Variety Only (NOT Progression):**

- Different story types (Mixed vs. Focus) for variety
- Different topics for variety
- Different grammatical emphasis (Past, Future, or Comparative focus)
- NO "integration phase" or "mastery phase"

This makes infinite content generation possible for a real skill.
