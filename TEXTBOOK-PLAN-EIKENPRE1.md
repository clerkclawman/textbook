## 4. Grammar Requirements per Story (CRITICAL)

### Discourse Markers: 6-8 Instances (STRICT MINIMUM = 6)

**CRITICAL: Discourse markers are THE key tested feature for Pre-1**

- **Target:** 6-8 instances per story
- **Minimum:** 6 discourse markers required (do NOT use fewer than 6)
- **Maximum:** 8 discourse markers to avoid over-use
- **Focus weeks:** Emphasise specific marker types (Addition, Contrast, Consequence)
- **MIXED weeks:** Include variety of all discourse types

**Discourse Marker Examples:**
- *"Moreover, universities are expanding online programmes."* (Addition)
- *"However, some students prefer traditional classrooms."* (Contrast)
- *"Therefore, hybrid models are becoming popular."* (Consequence)
- *"In conclusion, balance is essential."* (Conclusion)

### Academic Register: Formal Tone (ALL Stories)

**Elements required in ALL stories:**
- **Objective language:** *Research suggests* (not *I think*)
- **Nominalisation:** Verbs ‚Üí nouns (Week 8 focus)
- **Latinate vocabulary:** *examine, investigate, demonstrate*
- **Academic passive:** *It is argued that...*, *Studies have shown...* (Week 8 focus)

**Academic vs. Non-Academic:**
- Non-academic: "I think this problem is bad."
- Academic: "This issue presents significant challenges."

### All Sentences: 10-15 Words Maximum (TV-Readable Constraint)
- **Average:** 10-15 words per sentence
- **Maximum:** 20 words per sentence (hard limit)
- **Apply to:** ALL 20 lines (both story sentences AND discussion questions)

### Grammar Complexity Strategy
Use 2-3 short sentences with discourse markers instead of deep embedding:

**Before (too long):** *"Furthermore, whilst evidence suggests that students benefit from online learning, some studies have shown that face-to-face interaction has advantages."* (21 words)

**After (TV-readable):** *"Furthermore, students benefit from online learning. Evidence suggests this is effective."* (11 + 5 = 16 words total, 2 sentences)

## 6. Content Quality Guidelines

### Primary Priority: Good, Readable Content
- **Above all:** Stories should be engaging and well-written
- **Readability:** 10-15 word sentences ensure TV readability with translations
- **Academic tone:** Professional but not dry, formal but accessible
- **Topic relevance:** Academic/professional themes (research, education, technology, environment, policy, etc.)

### Secondary Priority: Humour and Engagement
**Use humour where appropriate to make content memorable:**

**Appropriate humour themes:**
- Academic bureaucracy delays and absurdities
- Perfect research plans that inevitably change
- Student procrastination and deadline madness
- Group project dynamics (everyone loves group work)
- Conference presentation anxiety
- Reviewer feedback being politely brutal

**Humour principles:**
- Gentle wit that university students will recognise
- Irony about real academic situations
- Playful commentary students relate to
- **Avoid:** Sarcasm, cynicism, mocking, or mean-spirited content

**When to use humour:**
- Opening lines can grab attention
- Examples can be playful
- Conclusions can warm the tone
- **When to avoid:** Serious topics like inequality, discrimination, tragedy

### Tertiary Priority: Seasonal References (OPTIONAL, Not Required)
**Seasonal content is OPTIONAL ‚Äî do not force it**

**If using seasonal references:**
- Mention seasonal events only when they naturally relate to the topic
- Ensure seasonal events appear in their correct month:
  - Cherry blossoms: March-April
  - Christmas: December
  - Halloween: October
- Don't force seasonal connections that feel artificial

**Better non-seasonal than weak seasonal:**
- ‚úÖ Excellent non-seasonal content about climate research
- ‚ùå Weak seasonal reference about Christmas in July (forced and confusing)

### Topic Flexibility
**Stories can be about ANY academic/professional topic:**
- Academic research and methodology
- Higher education systems
- Scientific inquiry and evidence
- Technology development and ethics
- Environmental studies and sustainability
- Public policy and governance
- International relations and diplomacy
- Economic analysis and markets
- Social sciences and society
- Medical research and healthcare
- Humanities, arts, philosophy
- Global issues and challenges

**Quality principle:** Theme flexibility allows creators to choose topics they can write well about

## Appendix B: Academic Register Elements

### Formal Language Elements

| Element | Description | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| **Objective language** | Avoid personal pronouns (*I think*) | ‚ùå "I think this problem is bad." ‚úÖ "This issue presents significant challenges." |
| **Nominalisation** | Verbs ‚Üí nouns | *decide ‚Üí decision, argue ‚Üí argument, evaluate ‚Üí evaluation* |
| **Latinate vocabulary** | Formal alternatives | *examine* vs. *look at*, *investigate* vs. *check* |
| **Academic passive** | Remove agent for objectivity | *It is argued that...*, *Studies have shown...* |
| **Tentative language** | Qualified claims | *suggests, indicates, appears to* (not absolute statements) |

### Week 8 Special Focus: Academic Register

**In Week 8 of each month (Stories 8, 16, 24, 32, 40, 48), emphasise:**
1. **Passive voice** for objectivity: *"The study was conducted...", "Results are reported..."*
2. **Nominalisation** for formal tone: *"The evaluation revealed...", "The implementation required..."*
3. **Latinate vocabulary** throughout: *"examine, investigate, demonstrate, analyse"*
4. **Formal linking phrases:** *"With regard to..., In terms of..., In light of..."*
