**Unlike previous levels:**

4. **Academic topics** ‚Äî Environment, science, politics, not just everyday situations
5. **Abstract reasoning** ‚Äî Hypothetical scenarios and theoretical discussions

### Three Pillars of Grade 2 Success

#### 1. Mixed Conditionals (CRITICAL ‚Äî New at Grade 2)

**What the data shows:**
- Ê∫ñ2Á¥ö„Éó„É©„Çπ introduces 2nd conditionals (*If I were*), but keeps them separate
- Grade 2 COMBINES conditionals: past condition + present result, present condition + past result
- This is tested in reading passages and listening contexts requiring complex temporal reasoning

**Test evidence:**
- Ê∫ñ2Á¥ö: 1st conditionals (*If...will*), 2nd conditionals (*If I were...*) separately
- Ê∫ñ2Á¥ö„Éó„É©„Çπ: 2nd conditionals established, but not mixed
- 2Á¥ö: **Mixed conditionals appear** (*If I had studied, I would be happy now*)

**Core patterns:**
1. **Past condition, present result:** *If I had studied harder, I would be at university now.*
2. **Present condition, past result:** *If I were more organised, I would have finished on time.*
3. **Past condition, past result (3rd conditional):** *If I had known, I would have told you.*

#### 2. Advanced Reported Speech (Core Extension from P2Plus)

**Continuing features from P2Plus:**
- Basic backshifting (present ‚Üí past, past ‚Üí past perfect)
- Time and place expressions (now ‚Üí then, here ‚Üí there, tomorrow ‚Üí the next day)
- Pronoun changes (I ‚Üí he/she, we ‚Üí they, my ‚Üí his/her)

**Grade 2 additions:**
- **Multi-level reported speech:** *She said that he told her that he would arrive.*
- **Reported questions:** *He asked me if I was coming.*
- **Reported commands/requests:** *She told him to wait.*
- **Mixed tenses in complex reporting:** *He mentioned that he had been planning the trip for months.*

**Academic applications:**
- Research summaries (*The study concluded that...)
- Literature reviews (*Researchers have found that...)
- Policy statements (*The government announced that...)

#### 3. Noun Clauses (CRITICAL ‚Äî New at Grade 2)

**What the data shows:**
- Noun clauses tested as subjects, objects, and complements
- Essential for expressing abstract thoughts and hypothetical scenarios
- Common in academic writing and professional reports

**Test evidence:**
- Ê∫ñ2Á¥ö„Éó„É©„Çπ: Minimal noun clause usage
- 2Á¥ö: **Noun clauses appear regularly** in reading passages and comprehension questions

**Core patterns:**
1. **Question words:** *What he said was surprising.* (subject)
2. **That-clauses:** *I believe that climate change is serious.* (object)
3. **If/whether:** *I don't know if it will rain.* (object)
4. **Wh-clauses as object:** *She explained why she left early.*

**Academic applications:**
- Problem statements (*What concerns researchers is...)
- Thesis statements (*This paper argues that...)
- Hypotheses (*Whether this approach works is uncertain.*

### The Academic Scope Expansion

**Current curriculum gap:**
- Many Grade 2 resources focus on everyday situations (shopping, travel, work)
- Actual exams include significant academic content (environment, science, politics)
- Students lack vocabulary for abstract academic topics

**Evidence from analysis:**
- Grade 2 vocabulary (2,447 words) includes academic terminology from these domains
- Reading passages discuss environmental issues, scientific research, policy debates
- Teaching priority should reflect: Academic topics > Everyday situations

**Domains to emphasise:**
1. **Environment:** Climate change, sustainability, pollution, conservation
2. **Science:** Technology, research methods, scientific discoveries
3. **Politics:** Government policies, elections, international relations
4. **Society:** Education, healthcare, economics, social issues

## 6. Seasonal & Cultural Context

### Overview

This section provides seasonal and cultural context for the Grade 2 curriculum. Seasonal references are **OPTIONAL**, not mandatory.

**Primary Goals:**
1. **Good, readable content** ‚Äî Most important
2. **Engaging, relatable material** ‚Äî Humour is ideal when appropriate
3. **Seasonal alignment** ‚Äî Optional; if included, must align to correct month

**For Grade 2:** Stories should focus on academic topics (education, environment, science, politics, technology). Seasonal events are nice extras when they fit naturally, but should never be forced.

**Content Quality Principles:**
- Better to have excellent non-seasonal content than weak seasonal references
- Humour and engagement are more valuable than seasonal accuracy
- Students learn better when content is interesting and relatable
- Seasonal events create memorable context when used naturally, but not required

### Seasonal Alignment Policy (SIMPLIFIED)

**Seasonal Content: OPTIONAL, Not Required**

Stories may include seasonal references, but this is not mandatory:
- **Primary Priority:** Good, readable, engaging content
- **Secondary Priority:** Humour and wit make content memorable
- **Tertiary Priority:** Seasonal alignment (if relevant to topic)

**When Using Seasonal References:**
- Mention seasonal events only when they naturally relate to the topic
- Ensure seasonal events appear in their correct month
- Don't force seasonal connections that feel artificial

**Tone and Engagement (IDEAL Quality Factors):**

**Primary Tone:** Academic but accessible, engaging and relevant

**Humour (Ideal to Include Where Appropriate):**
- Use gentle wit that students will recognise
- Irony about academic situations (study stress, deadlines, group projects)
- Playful commentary students relate to (exam preparation, library adventures)
- **Avoid:** Sarcasm, cynicism, mocking, or mean-spirited content

**Engagement Characteristics:**
- Relatable situations students experience
- Memorable examples that stick
- Balanced tone: academic but not dry, engaging but not silly
- Humour should be light, warm, and inclusive

**Example Humor Themes:**
- Exam preparation struggles and last-minute cramming
- Group project dynamics (everyone LOVES group work)
- Library adventures and book hunt challenges
- Lost homework or forgotten assignment panics
- Classroom technology failures (projector crisis!)
- Confusing academic jargon decoded

**When to Use Humour:**
- Opening lines can grab attention
- Examples can be playful
- Conclusions can warm the tone
- **When to avoid:** Serious topics like environmental disasters, political conflicts

## 7. Writing Guidelines ‚Äî Sentence Length, Vocabulary, Complexity

### Sentence Length Requirements (TV-Readable)

| Metric | Target | Notes |
|--------|--------|-------|
| **Average sentence length** | 10-15 words per sentence | **TV-readable with translations** |
| **Minimum sentence length** | 8 words | Avoid fragmentation |
| **Maximum sentence length** | 20 words | Strict limit for readability |
| **Sentence length variety** | 8-15 word range | Keep natural flow |
| **Percentage of sentences** (15+ words) | 20-30% | Occasional longer sentences only |

**Critical Constraint:** All sentences must be TV-readable with Japanese translations displayed simultaneously. This constraint applies to **all Eiken levels** (5Á¥ö through 2Á¥ö).

**Note:** These targets are for individual sentences (lines 1-10), not total story word count. Each story contains exactly 20 lines. whilst actual Grade 2 exams use 18-22 word average sentences, this curriculum uses 10-15 words for TV readability.

### Sentence Length Strategy (Maintaining Grammar Complexity)

**How to maintain Grade 2 complexity without long sentences:**

1. **Use short sentences with conditionals** instead of long embedding
   - *Instead of:* "If the researchers had conducted the study more carefully, they would have discovered the error earlier, which would have saved time." (25 words)
   - *Use:* "If researchers had been more careful, they would have found the error. This would have saved time." (16 words across 2 sentences)

2. **Separate reported speech clauses**
   - *Instead of:* "She told me that she believed the government would announce the new policy next month, which surprised everyone." (21 words)
   - *Use:* "She told me she believed the government would announce the policy. This surprised everyone." (15 words across 2 sentences)

3. **Use discourse connectors for noun clauses**
